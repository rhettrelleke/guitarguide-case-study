# Design Decisions

## 1. Treat Progression Building As The Center Of The Product
Decision: organize the experience around turning chord ideas into progressions worth keeping.

Why: for many players, songs start to become real when chords begin to relate to each other, not when a single shape is identified.

Tradeoff: this narrows the product and leaves some adjacent music-tool use cases intentionally underserved.

What it enabled: a clearer product story and a more coherent writing workflow.

## 2. Keep The Product Focused
Decision: avoid turning GuitarGuide into a broad, all-in-one music platform.

Why: focus creates trust. Users understand the product faster when it has a visible center.

Tradeoff: some feature opportunities become easier to reject than to pursue.

What it enabled: better UX discipline and a more distinct product identity.

## 3. Make Playback A Core Part Of Evaluation
Decision: treat hearing ideas back as part of the writing loop rather than as a secondary utility.

Why: players need fast musical feedback to decide whether a direction is worth continuing.

Tradeoff: experiences built around feedback require more care than experiences built only around reference.

What it enabled: faster iteration and more confidence during progression building.

## 4. Treat Tuning Context As First-Class
Decision: consider tuning as part of the writing workflow instead of a peripheral customization.

Why: for many players, tuning changes what feels playable, musical, and worth exploring.

Tradeoff: supporting this well adds product complexity, so it has to justify itself through real user value.

What it enabled: a product that better reflects how many guitar players actually write.

## 5. Keep The Free Experience Legitimately Useful
Decision: make the free product capable enough to stand on its own.

Why: useful products earn trust before they ask for commitment.

Tradeoff: the premium story has to be built on depth and convenience rather than on frustration.

What it enabled: a healthier relationship between product quality and monetization.

## 6. Prefer Clarity Over Exhaustiveness In Guidance
Decision: product guidance should help users move forward without overwhelming them.

Why: too many options can feel less helpful than a smaller set of better-framed options.

Tradeoff: the product has to choose what not to surface.

What it enabled: a calmer experience and stronger confidence during exploration.

## 7. Design For Momentum, Not Just Capability
Decision: evaluate features partly by whether they help users stay in a creative state.

Why: capability without flow often creates impressive demos but weak real use.

Tradeoff: some technically interesting ideas are not worth shipping if they interrupt the writing experience.

What it enabled: a product that is easier to return to and easier to trust.
